Seafarers during the pandemic

The plea for humanitarian treatment of seafarers continues

On July 8th, ships sounded their horns for the second time during the pandemic to raise awareness of the need to facilitate crew changes. The number of seafarers stranded on ships or waiting on shore to go aboard rose in the past month. International maritime organizations called for the adoption of industry guidelines and government cooperation, but few followed through.
India, along with the Philippines, did open ports to crew change, starting with Dubai. The flag administration of Cyprus has also maintained an active role in aiding crew change efforts, starting with imposing specific protocols for ships in Cyprus and adopting remote techniques to do part of their work.
Even when implementing stringent safety measures, seafarers during crew changes are still vulnerable to COVID-19. There have been various outbreaks on vessels near Spain, Algeciras, and even Argentina where almost every crewmember on a a fishing vessel tested positive for COVID-19 despite extensive quarantining and testing. Still, companies continue the effort to relieve seafarers. Maersk experienced a successful crew change in Panama for the second time during the pandemic and plans to do more.
