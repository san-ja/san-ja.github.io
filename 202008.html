Pleas left unheard

Crew change challenges persist

Although horns flared and the numbers of seafarers stuck at sea has risen to approximately 300,000, countries and most small companies are slow to make changes that would relieve seafarers stucks at ships long past the end of their contracts. Some seafarers are even refusing to sail with the support of the ITF. Reinstatements of restrictions at ports are making it even harder to perform crew changes. Charters are discriminating against ships with long-serving crews onboard.
On the bright side, India, the Philippines, and Jamaica continue to make progress. India has enabled more than 100,000 crew changes and launched initiatives like e-learning, an online utility to facilitate travel, and online Exit Exams. The Philippines opened up a "green lane" to allow for speedy and safe travels of seafarers with guidelines from MARINA. Paired with the the opening of three more ports, the Philippines aims to become a global hub for crew changes. Jamaica lists seafarers as "essential workers" and helps facilitate crew changes. Even individual companies are stepping up to improve working conditions.
In the meantime, the IMO released new protocols to keep seafarers safe and BIMCO and the ICS are preparing for the shipping industry in a post-COVID world. For COVID-19 resources, watch this video by ISWAN to answer your questions and browse these resources collected by Nautilus International.
